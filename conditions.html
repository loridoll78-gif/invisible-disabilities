Lupus (Systemic Lupus Erythematosus)

Autoimmune & Chronic Illness

üìñ What is it?

Lupus is a chronic autoimmune disease where your immune system attacks your own healthy tissues and organs. It's like having your body's defense system turn against you - it can affect your skin, joints, kidneys, heart, lungs, blood vessels, and brain. Lupus is unpredictable and can range from mild to life-threatening.

üëÅÔ∏è Why is it "invisible"?

People with lupus often look completely healthy, but their bodies are experiencing:

  • Extreme fatigue that feels like being hit by a truck
  • Joint pain and swelling that migrates throughout the body
  • Muscle aches and weakness
  • Recurring fevers and feeling generally unwell
  • Brain fog affecting memory, concentration, and decision-making
  • Sensitivity to sunlight that can trigger flares
  • Hair loss and skin rashes (including the butterfly rash across cheeks)
  • Kidney problems that can be life-threatening
  • Blood clotting issues and circulation problems

üìÖ The daily reality:

  • Waking up feeling like you worked out for hours when you did nothing
  • Planning your entire life around avoiding sun exposure
  • Having "good days" followed by weeks of debilitating flares
  • Taking handfuls of medications just to function
  • Constantly monitoring symptoms for signs of organ involvement
  • Looking healthy while your immune system wages war inside
  • Living with the uncertainty of never knowing how you'll feel tomorrow

‚ùå Common misconceptions:

‚ùå "You look fine, you must be feeling better" ‚úì Lupus symptoms fluctuate; looking good doesn't mean feeling good
‚ùå "Just get some sun, it'll help" ‚úì Sun exposure can trigger dangerous flares
‚ùå "It's not that serious" ‚úì Lupus can be life-threatening and cause permanent organ damage

üíú What people with lupus want you to know:

  • ‚úÖ Flares are unpredictable - we can't control when they happen
  • ‚úÖ We may need to cancel plans last minute when symptoms spike
  • ‚úÖ Fatigue isn't just being tired - it's complete physical and mental exhaustion
  • ‚úÖ We live with constant fear of organ damage and disease progression
  • ‚úÖ Medications have serious side effects, but they're necessary to stay alive

ü§ù How you can help:

  • Understand that lupus is unpredictable and plans may need to change
  • Don't take our limitations or cancelled plans personally
  • Help us avoid triggers like UV exposure when possible
  • Offer specific help: "Can I drive you to your appointment?" instead of "Let me know if you need anything"
  • Learn about lupus so you can better understand our experience
  • Be patient when brain fog affects our memory or communication
Lupus affects at least 1.5 million Americans and 5 million people worldwide. It strikes mostly women (90%) typically between ages 15-45, but can affect anyone.
üíú To our lupus warriors: Your fight is real, your strength is incredible, and your journey matters. You are not alone. üíú

Rheumatoid Arthritis

Autoimmune & Chronic Illness

üìñ What is it?

Rheumatoid arthritis is a chronic autoimmune disease where your immune system mistakenly attacks the lining of your joints, causing painful inflammation. Unlike the "wear and tear" arthritis people think of, RA is your body's defense system gone rogue - attacking healthy joint tissue and potentially organs like your heart, lungs, and eyes. It's progressive and can cause permanent joint damage and deformity if untreated.

üëÅÔ∏è Why is it "invisible"?

People with RA often appear normal, but their bodies are experiencing:

  • Excruciating joint pain that feels like broken glass in your joints
  • Morning stiffness lasting 1-6 hours (not just a few minutes)
  • Crushing fatigue from constant immune system activation
  • Symmetrical joint swelling (both hands, both feet, etc.)
  • Flu-like symptoms during flares- Brain fog and difficulty concentrating
  • Low-grade fevers and feeling generally unwell
  • Muscle weakness and loss of grip strength - Depression and anxiety from chronic pain and uncertainty

üìÖ The daily reality:

  • Waking up with joints so stiff you can't make a fist or walk normally
  • Taking 30+ minutes to "loosen up" enough to function each morning
  • Using both hands to open a water bottle or struggling with doorknobs
  • Planning activities around your "good" and "bad" days
  • Taking powerful medications that suppress your immune system
  • Living with constant fear of permanent joint damage
  • Looking fine while feeling like your joints are on fire
  • Having "invisible" swelling that others can't see but you definitely feel

‚ùå Common misconceptions:

‚ùå "That's old people arthritis" ‚úì RA commonly strikes people in their 30s-50s, even children
‚ùå "Just take some ibuprofen" ‚úì RA requires powerful disease-modifying drugs, not just pain relievers
‚ùå "You look fine today!" ‚úì Good days don't mean the disease is gone
‚ùå "My grandma had arthritis too" ‚úì RA is autoimmune, very different from osteoarthritis
‚ùå "At least it's just joint pain" ‚úì RA can damage organs and significantly shorten life expectancy
‚ùå "Exercise will cure it" ‚úì While helpful, there's no cure for RA
‚ùå "You're too young and healthy-looking" ‚úì RA doesn't discriminate by age or appearance

üíú What people with RA want you to know:

  • ‚úÖ Morning stiffness isn't just being "slow to wake up" - our joints literally seize up overnight
  • ‚úÖ We take chemotherapy drugs (methotrexate) and immune suppressants to function
  • ‚úÖ "But you look so good!" isn't always a compliment - it dismisses our invisible struggle
  • ‚úÖ We live with constant fear of flares and progressive joint damage
  • ‚úÖ Fatigue isn't just tiredness - it's complete physical and mental exhaustion
  • ‚úÖ We've become masters at hiding pain and adapting to limitations
  • ‚úÖ Every day is different - what we can do today might not be possible tomorrow
  • ‚úÖ We're not "giving up" when we use mobility aids - we're being smart about joint protection

ü§ù How you can help:

  • Understand that stiffness and pain are worst in the mornings
  • Don't offer firm handshakes or expect us to open jars/bottles
  • Be patient when we move slowly or need extra time
  • Offer specific help: "I'll carry that for you" instead of "Let me know if you need help"
  • Don't judge our medication choices - they're necessary to prevent disability
  • Accept that we may need to cancel plans when flares hit
  • Learn about RA so you understand it's not just "aches and pains"
  • Celebrate our good days without expecting them to be permanent
Rheumatoid arthritis affects approximately 1.3 million Americans, with women being 3 times more likely to develop it than men. While it can strike at any age, it most commonly begins between ages 30-60. RA is more than joint pain - it's a systemic disease that can affect your entire body and quality of life.
üíú To our RA warriors in our community: Your pain is real, your strength is extraordinary, and your fight matters. You are not your diagnosis. You are not alone in this battle. üíú

Psoriatic spondyloarthritis (PsA)

Autoimmune & Chronic Illness

üìñ What is it?

Psoriatic Arthritis is a chronic autoimmune disease that combines inflammatory arthritis with psoriasis (a skin condition). It occurs when your immune system attacks healthy joints, tendons, ligaments, and skin, causing painful inflammation and progressive damage. Unlike osteoarthritis (wear-and-tear arthritis), PsA is an autoimmune condition where your body literally attacks itself. Not everyone with psoriasis develops PsA, and some people develop arthritis symptoms before any skin symptoms appear. It can affect any joint in the body and often causes debilitating fatigue and systemic inflammation.

üëÅÔ∏è Why is it "invisible"?

People with psoriatic arthritis often look completely normal, especially when clothed, but their bodies are experiencing:

  • Severe joint pain, stiffness, and swelling that fluctuates unpredictably
  • Crushing fatigue that feels like your entire body is shutting down
  • Enthesitis (inflammation where tendons attach to bone) causing pain in unexpected places
  • Dactylitis ("sausage digits") - entire fingers or toes swelling like sausages
  • Nail changes including pitting, crumbling, or separation from nail bed
  • Skin plaques that may be hidden under clothing
  • Morning stiffness lasting hours, making it impossible to function immediately after waking
  • Brain fog and cognitive issues from chronic inflammation
  • Eye inflammation (uveitis) that can threaten vision
  • Spine and lower back pain from inflammatory changes

üìÖ The daily reality:

Physical Symptoms:

  • Waking up with joints so stiff and painful you can't make a fist or walk normally
  • Needing 2-3 hours just to "loosen up" enough to start your day
  • Experiencing pain that migrates between different joints without warning
  • Having your feet hurt so badly that walking feels like stepping on broken glass
  • Dealing with swollen fingers that won't bend or grip properly
  • Chronic lower back pain that makes sitting, standing, or lying down uncomfortable
  • Fatigue so overwhelming that showering requires a nap afterward

Medication Management:

  • Taking powerful immunosuppressants or biologics with serious side effects
  • Getting frequent injections or IV infusions just to function
  • Being vulnerable to infections because your immune system is suppressed
  • Dealing with medication side effects like nausea, headaches, or injection site reactions
  • Fighting insurance companies for coverage of expensive biologic medications
  • Living with the fear that your current medication will stop working

Social Impact:

  • Canceling plans when a flare hits without warning
  • Having people dismiss your condition as "just arthritis" or "just a skin problem"
  • Feeling self-conscious about visible skin plaques or nail changes
  • Being unable to participate in physical activities you once loved
  • Struggling with intimacy due to pain, fatigue, and skin symptoms
  • Losing jobs or career opportunities due to unpredictable symptoms

Emotional Toll:

  • Grieving your former active, pain-free life
  • Dealing with anxiety about disease progression and joint damage
  • Depression from chronic pain and lifestyle limitations
  • Frustration when others don't understand the severity of your condition
  • Fear of becoming disabled or losing independence
  • Stress from managing a complex, lifelong autoimmune disease

‚ùå Common misconceptions:

‚ùå "It's just a skin condition with some joint pain" ‚úì PsA is a serious systemic autoimmune disease that can cause permanent joint damage and affect multiple organs
‚ùå "Just take some ibuprofen" ‚úì PsA requires disease-modifying drugs to prevent progressive joint destruction
‚ùå "You're too young for arthritis" ‚úì PsA typically develops between ages 30-50, often in prime working years
‚ùå "At least it's not rheumatoid arthritis" ‚úì PsA can be just as severe and disabling as RA
‚ùå "Your skin doesn't look that bad" ‚úì The severity of skin symptoms doesn't correlate with arthritis severity
‚ùå "You seemed fine yesterday" ‚úì PsA symptoms fluctuate dramatically day to day or even hour to hour

üíú What people with psoriatic-spondyloarthritis want you to know:

  • ‚úÖ Our pain is real and often severe - we're not exaggerating or being dramatic
  • ‚úÖ We take chemotherapy-type drugs just to function - biologics and immunosuppressants have serious risks
  • ‚úÖ Looking good doesn't mean feeling good - we've mastered hiding our pain
  • ‚úÖ We can't "push through" flares - forcing activity during flares causes more joint damage
  • ‚úÖ Morning stiffness isn't just being "slow to wake up" - our joints literally seize up overnight
  • ‚úÖ We live with constant fear of permanent disability - untreated or poorly controlled PsA causes irreversible damage
  • ‚úÖ Cold weather, stress, and infections make everything worse - we're not being dramatic about triggers
  • ‚úÖ We're not lazy - we're fighting an autoimmune disease that attacks our joints and drains our energy
  • ‚úÖ Some days we need mobility aids, other days we don't - that's the nature of inflammatory arthritis

ü§ù How you can help:

Understanding and Support:

  • Believe us when we say we're in pain or too exhausted to participate
  • Don't compare PsA to normal aches and pains or "regular" arthritis
  • Understand that we may need to cancel plans when flares hit
  • Be patient when morning stiffness makes us slow or need extra time
  • Don't judge our medication choices - they're necessary to prevent disability

Practical Help:

  • Offer to help with tasks requiring grip strength (opening jars, carrying heavy items)
  • Choose activities and venues that are joint-friendly (comfortable seating, minimal walking)
  • Help with household tasks during severe flares
  • Be understanding about temperature preferences (cold often worsens symptoms)
  • Support our need for frequent breaks or position changes

Advocacy:

  • Challenge the misconception that arthritis is just "aches and pains"
  • Support research funding for autoimmune diseases
  • Advocate for insurance coverage of biologic medications
  • Help spread awareness that PsA is a serious, systemic condition
  • Don't minimize our experience by comparing it to minor joint pain

Medical Support:

  • Encourage early diagnosis and treatment (delays cause permanent damage)
  • Support our need for rheumatology care and regular monitoring
  • Understand that finding the right medication can take time and multiple tries
  • Be patient during medication transitions when symptoms may worsen temporarily

Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

Autoimmune & Chronic Illness

üìñ What is it?

Multiple Sclerosis is a chronic autoimmune disease where your immune system attacks the protective coating (myelin) around nerve fibers in your brain and spinal cord. Think of myelin like the insulation on electrical wires - when it's damaged, signals between your brain and body get scrambled, delayed, or lost entirely. MS is unpredictable and progressive, with symptoms that can affect virtually any part of your body. There are different types of MS, but all involve your nervous system being under constant attack by your own immune system.

üëÅÔ∏è Why is it "invisible"?

People with MS often look completely healthy, but their bodies are experiencing:

  • Crushing fatigue that feels like your battery is permanently at 5%
  • Cognitive fog affecting memory, concentration, and processing speed
  • Numbness, tingling, or burning sensations throughout the body
  • Muscle weakness and coordination problems
  • Heat sensitivity that makes warm weather debilitating
  • Balance issues and dizziness
  • Vision problems including double vision and blind spots
  • Pain that ranges from sharp shooting pains to constant aching
  • Bladder and bowel dysfunction- Speech difficulties and swallowing problems

üìÖ The daily reality:

  • Planning your entire day around energy levels and heat exposure
  • Feeling like you're walking through quicksand when others move normally
  • Having your body "short circuit" in ways that change daily
  • Needing to rest after activities others find simple
  • Avoiding hot showers, warm weather, and heated spaces
  • Taking expensive medications that suppress your immune system
  • Living with the fear of relapses and disease progression
  • Losing words mid-sentence or forgetting what you were doing
  • Looking fine while feeling like your nervous system is malfunctioning

‚ùå Common misconceptions:

‚ùå "You don't look sick" ‚úì MS symptoms are mostly invisible and fluctuate daily
‚ùå "MS means you'll end up in a wheelchair" ‚úì Most people with MS never need wheelchairs
‚ùå "You're too young to have MS" ‚úì MS typically develops between ages 20-50
‚ùå "Everyone with MS is the same" ‚úì MS affects everyone differently
‚ùå "You seemed fine yesterday" ‚úì MS symptoms change hour by hour, day by day
‚ùå "At least it's not terminal" ‚úì MS can significantly shorten lifespan and severely impact quality of life
‚ùå "Just push through the fatigue" ‚úì MS fatigue is neurological, not regular tiredness

üíú What people with MS want you to know:

  • ‚úÖ Our fatigue isn't laziness - it's our nervous system failing to conduct signals properly
  • ‚úÖ We may cancel plans last minute when symptoms flare unexpectedly
  • ‚úÖ Heat makes everything worse - we're not being dramatic about temperature
  • ‚úÖ Cognitive symptoms are real - we're not "losing our minds"
  • ‚úÖ We live with constant uncertainty about our future abilities
  • ‚úÖ Good days don't mean we're getting better - MS is unpredictable
  • ‚úÖ We take powerful medications with serious side effects to slow progression
  • ‚úÖ We grieve the loss of our former selves while trying to adapt to new limitations

ü§ù How you can help:

  • Understand that our energy is limited and must be carefully rationed
  • Don't judge us for using mobility aids on bad days but not others
  • Keep spaces cool when possible and understand our heat sensitivity
  • Be patient when cognitive fog affects our memory or speech
  • Don't make assumptions about what we can or can't do based on appearance
  • Learn about MS so you can better understand our daily challenges
  • Support us in pacing activities and not overcommitting
  • Celebrate our good days while understanding they may not last
Multiple Sclerosis affects approximately 1 million Americans, with women being 2-3 times more likely to develop it than men. It's typically diagnosed between ages 20-50, often during peak career and family-building years. While MS is progressive, many people live full lives with proper treatment and support.

Type 1 Diabetes (T1D)

Autoimmune & Chronic Illness

üìñ What is it?

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune disease where your immune system destroys the insulin-producing cells in your pancreas. Unlike Type 2 diabetes, it's not caused by lifestyle choices - it's your body attacking itself. Without insulin, glucose can't enter your cells, so your blood sugar becomes dangerously high. People with T1D must inject insulin multiple times daily or use an insulin pump just to stay alive. It's a 24/7, life-or-death medical condition with no breaks, no cure, and no days off.

üëÅÔ∏è Why is it "invisible"?

People with T1D often look completely healthy, but their bodies are constantly experiencing:

  • Blood sugar swings that feel like emotional and physical roller coasters
  • Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) that can cause confusion, shakiness, and loss of consciousness
  • Hyperglycemia (high blood sugar) causing fatigue, nausea, and feeling "toxic"
  • Constant mental calculations about food, insulin, and activity
  • Sleep disruption from blood sugar alarms and nighttime management
  • Physical exhaustion from managing a complex medical condition 24/7
  • Anxiety and stress from the life-threatening nature of blood sugar extremes
  • Long-term complications affecting eyes, kidneys, nerves, and heart
  • The emotional burden of never being able to "take a break" from their condition

üìÖ The daily reality:

  • Checking blood sugar 8-12+ times per day, every day
  • Calculating carbs, protein, and fat for every meal and snack
  • Carrying medical supplies everywhere - glucose meters, insulin, snacks, emergency glucagon
  • Setting alarms throughout the night to check blood sugars
  • Having to eat when you're not hungry or delay meals based on blood sugar
  • Explaining to others that you can eat sugar/carbs, but it requires precise insulin dosing
  • Living with the constant awareness that a miscalculation could be dangerous
  • Managing insurance battles for life-sustaining supplies and technology
  • Wearing or carrying medical devices like continuous glucose monitors and insulin pumps

‚ùå Common misconceptions:

‚ùå "You can't eat sugar/carbs" ‚úì T1Ds can eat anything with proper insulin dosing
‚ùå "You caused this with your diet" ‚úì T1D is autoimmune, not lifestyle-related
‚ùå "Just take your insulin and you're fine" ‚úì Blood sugar management is incredibly complex
‚ùå "It's not that serious, you look great" ‚úì T1D can cause life-threatening emergencies and serious complications.
‚ùå "You're lucky you don't have to worry about your weight" ‚úì T1D doesn't prevent weight gain and can actually make weight management harder.
‚ùå "Can't you just check your blood sugar less often?" ‚úì Frequent monitoring prevents dangerous highs and lows.
‚ùå "At least it's manageable now with technology" ‚úì Technology helps but doesn't eliminate the 24/7 burden

üíú What people with T1D want you to know:

  • ‚úÖ We're doing complex medical calculations every time we eat.
  • ‚úÖ Our mood and energy levels are directly affected by blood sugar levels.
  • ‚úÖ We can't just "take a break" from managing our diabetes.
  • ‚úÖ Low blood sugars are medical emergencies that require immediate treatment.
  • ‚úÖ We live with constant fear of dangerous highs, lows, and long-term complications
  • ‚úÖ Technology like CGMs and pumps help but don't cure or eliminate daily management
  • ‚úÖ We can do anything others can do, but it requires extra planning and preparation
  • ‚úÖ Burnout from constant medical management is real and valid

ü§ù How you can help:

  • Learn the signs of high and low blood sugar emergencies
  • Don't police our food choices or comment on what we're eating
  • Be patient when we need to check blood sugar or take insulin during activities
  • Understand that we may need to eat at specific times or delay plans.
  • Keep glucose tablets or juice on hand if we're together frequently.
  • Don't make assumptions about what we can or can't do.
  • Support us in advocating for insurance coverage of necessary supplies.
  • Recognize that managing T1D is exhausting and celebrate our daily perseverance
Type 1 diabetes affects approximately 1.6 million Americans, including about 200,000 children. It can develop at any age, though it's often diagnosed in childhood or young adulthood. T1D requires constant vigilance and has no cure - only daily management to prevent life-threatening complications.
üíô To our T1D warriors in our community: Your daily strength in managing this relentless condition is extraordinary. You are more than your blood sugar numbers. You are not defined by your diabetes. You are not alone. üíô

Hashimoto's Thyroiditis

Autoimmune & Chronic Illness

üìñ What is it?

Hashimoto's disease is an autoimmune condition where the immune system attacks the thyroid gland, gradually destroying its ability to produce hormones. Think of your thyroid as your body's engine control center - when it's under attack, everything slows down and malfunctions.

üëÅÔ∏è Why is it "invisible"?

People with Hashimoto's often appear perfectly healthy, but their bodies are dealing with:

  • Extreme fatigue that rest doesn't fix.
  • Unexplained weight gain despite eating well.
  • Brain fog and memory problems
  • Depression, anxiety, and mood swings.
  • Hair loss and brittle nails.
  • Feeling cold all the time.
  • Joint and muscle pain.
  • Digestive issues and bloating.
  • Sleep problems despite exhaustion

üìÖ The daily reality:

  • Waking up exhausted even after 10+ hours of sleep
  • Simple tasks feeling overwhelming due to brain fog
  • Your body temperature regulation being completely off
  • Watching your hair fall out in clumps
  • People thinking you're "lazy" when you're actually struggling to function
  • Medication adjustments being a constant balancing act
  • Good days and bad days varying dramatically

‚ùå Common misconceptions:

‚ùå "Just take a pill and you'll be fine" ‚úì Treatment is complex and ongoing
‚ùå "You just need to exercise more" ‚úì Fatigue isn't laziness; it's a real physical symptom
‚ùå "It's just your thyroid, that's easy to fix" ‚úì Autoimmune diseases affect your whole body
‚ùå "You look fine to me" ‚úì Autoimmune symptoms are internal and fluctuating
‚ùå "Everyone gains weight as they age" ‚úì Hashimoto's weight gain is different and harder to control

üíú What people with Hashimoto's want you to know:

  • ‚úÖ Our energy levels aren't a choice - they're controlled by hormones
  • ‚úÖ "Just rest more" doesn't help when fatigue is hormonal, not from exertion
  • ‚úÖ Weight changes aren't from lack of willpower - our metabolism is compromised
  • ‚úÖ Brain fog is real - we're not being careless or forgetful on purpose
  • ‚úÖ Temperature sensitivity means we might need heat when you're comfortable
  • ‚úÖ We're managing a lifelong autoimmune condition, not just a simple hormone issue

ü§ù How you can help:

  • Understand that fatigue is a real, debilitating symptom
  • Be patient when we're having brain fog moments
  • Don't comment on weight changes - we're already struggling with body image

  • Brain fog affecting concentration and memory

Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD)

Sensory Disabilities

üìñ What is it?

Sensory Processing Disorder occurs when the nervous system receives sensory information but doesn't respond to it appropriately. People with SPD may be over-responsive (hypersensitive), under-responsive (hyposensitive), or crave sensory input. It affects how the brain processes information from the senses - touch, sound, sight, taste, smell, movement, and body position. SPD isn't just being "picky" or "sensitive" - it's a neurological condition where everyday sensory experiences can be overwhelming, painful, or barely registered at all. It significantly impacts daily functioning, relationships, and quality of life.

üëÅÔ∏è Why is it "invisible"?

People with SPD often look completely normal, but their nervous systems are experiencing:

  • Physical pain from textures, sounds, or lights that others find normal
  • Overwhelming anxiety in environments with multiple sensory inputs
  • Difficulty filtering background noise from important sounds
  • Feeling "too much" or "not enough" from sensory experiences
  • Exhaustion from constantly processing sensory information
  • Meltdowns or shutdowns when sensory systems become overloaded
  • Difficulty with coordination, balance, and body awareness
  • Social challenges when sensory needs conflict with social expectations
  • Constant vigilance about sensory triggers in their environment
  • Feeling misunderstood when their reactions seem "extreme" to others

üìÖ The daily reality:

  • Avoiding certain clothing textures, fabrics, or seams that feel unbearable
  • Being unable to concentrate in noisy or visually busy environments
  • Feeling overwhelmed in places like grocery stores, malls, or restaurants
  • Needing to leave social events early due to sensory overload
  • Having "sensory meltdowns" that look like tantrums but are neurological responses
  • Carefully planning outings around sensory factors like lighting and noise levels
  • Using coping strategies like noise-cancelling headphones or fidget items
  • Feeling judged for seeming "dramatic" about things others tolerate easily
  • Having others dismiss their needs as being "too sensitive" or "attention-seeking‚Äù

‚ùå Common misconceptions:

‚ùå "They're just being picky or dramatic" ‚úì SPD causes genuine neurological distress
‚ùå "They'll grow out of it" ‚úì SPD is a lifelong neurological difference
‚ùå "It's not a real disorder" ‚úì SPD significantly impacts daily functioning and quality of life
‚ùå "They just need to toughen up" ‚úì You can't "toughen up" a neurological processing difference
‚ùå "It only affects children" ‚úì Many adults have SPD but may be undiagnosed

‚ùå "It's just autism" ‚úì While they can co-occur, SPD can exist independently

Sensory Processing Disorder affects 5-16% of children and many adults, though adult SPD is often undiagnosed or misunderstood. Perimenopause

Special Topics

üìñ What is it?

Perimenopause is the natural transition period before menopause when your ovaries gradually produce less estrogen. While it's not a disability or disease, it's a significant biological process that can span 4-10 years and profoundly impact your physical, mental, and emotional well-being. Your hormones are on a rollercoaster, and your body is adjusting to massive changes - often with little warning or preparation.

üëÅÔ∏è Why is it "invisible"?

People going through perimenopause often appear completely normal, but their bodies are experiencing:

  • Debilitating hot flashes and night sweats that disrupt sleep and daily life
  • Severe brain fog affecting memory, focus, and decision-making
  • Crushing fatigue that isn't resolved by rest
  • Mood swings, anxiety, and depression that feel out of character
  • Joint aches and muscle pain
  • Heart palpitations and feeling "wired but tired"
  • Irregular, heavy, or painful periods
  • Sleep disruption and insomnia
  • Changes in weight and body composition
  • Digestive issues and food sensitivities

üìÖ The daily reality:

  • Waking up drenched in sweat multiple times per night

  • Being exhausted but unable to sleep

  • Navigating this major life transition with little support or understanding

Living with Mitrofanoff Stoma

Special Topics

üìñ What is it?

A Mitrofanoff is a surgical procedure that creates a small, continent (leak-proof) channel from the bladder to the skin surface, typically using the appendix. This creates a tiny opening called a stoma, usually located on the abdomen or in the belly button. People with a Mitrofanoff use a catheter to empty their bladder through this stoma several times a day. It's commonly done for people with a neurogenic bladder from conditions like spina bifida, spinal cord injuries, cerebral palsy, or other conditions that affect bladder control.

üëÅÔ∏è Why is it "invisible"?

The Mitrofanoff stoma and its daily management are completely hidden from view:

  • The stoma is tiny (pencil eraser size) and often hidden under clothing
  • Catheterization is done privately in bathrooms, so others never see the process
  • The constant need to carry catheter supplies isn't obvious to others
  • Timing life around catheterization schedules is invisible but restrictive
  • Potential complications like infections or blockages aren't visible
  • The emotional impact of having a stoma is internal
  • Many people don't know what a Mitrofanoff is, so the challenges go unrecognized
  • It allows continent bladder management without external bags or diapers

üìÖ The daily reality:

  • Catheterizing 4-6 times daily on a strict schedule to prevent complications
  • Always carrying a "catheter kit" with supplies wherever you go
  • Planning trips and activities around bathroom access and timing
  • Dealing with potential urinary tract infections from catheter use
  • Managing the anxiety of catheter blockages or stoma complications
  • Maintaining sterile technique to prevent infections
  • Explaining to partners, friends, or employers why bathroom breaks are non-negotiable
  • Living with the constant awareness of bladder management
  • Dealing with supply shortages or insurance coverage issues
  • Managing the physical and emotional adjustment to having a stoma

‚ùå Common misconceptions:

‚ùå "It's just like having a colostomy" ‚úì Mitrofanoff is for urine, not stool, and requires active catheterization
‚ùå "You can hold it like everyone else" ‚úì The bladder must be emptied on schedule to prevent dangerous complications
‚ùå "It's a cure for bladder problems" ‚úì It's bladder management, not a cure - it requires lifelong commitment
‚ùå "You can't live a normal life" ‚úì Many people with Mitrofanoff live full, active lives
‚ùå "It's always done in childhood" ‚úì Adults can have Mitrofanoff surgery for various conditions
‚ùå "It's just a minor inconvenience" ‚úì Proper Mitrofanoff management requires significant daily time and attention

üíú What people Living with Mitrofanoff Stoma want you to know:

  • ‚úÖ We may need to excuse ourselves frequently, and it's not negotiable
  • ‚úÖ We carry medical supplies with us everywhere we go
  • ‚úÖ Bathroom access is a genuine accessibility need for us
  • ‚úÖ We're not "lucky" to not have to use regular bathrooms - we still need them
  • ‚úÖ Our stoma is a medical device, not something to be curious about or comment on
  • ‚úÖ We may have "good days" and "bad days" with infections or complications

  • Don't ask invasive questions about our stoma or catheterization process
  • Respect our need for privacy during catheter changes

  • Help advocate for medical supply coverage and accessibility
  • Be patient if we need extra time or have to cancel due to complications
  • Treat us as capable individuals who happen to have different bathroom needs
  • Support research and awareness for conditions requiring Mitrofanoff

Living with multiple chronic conditions (also called multimorbidity or comorbidity) means managing two or more long-term health conditions simultaneously. These conditions often interact with each other in complex ways, creating a web of symptoms, treatments, and challenges that go far beyond the sum of their parts. Whether it's autoimmune conditions clustering together, mental health conditions alongside physical illness, or a combination of pain, fatigue, and neurological issues, multiple chronic conditions create a unique disability experience that's often misunderstood.

üëÅÔ∏è Why is it "invisible"?

The complexity of multiple conditions creates layers of invisible struggles:

  • Symptoms from different conditions overlapping and amplifying each other
  • Medication side effects that compound and interact unpredictably

  • Brain fog intensified by managing complex medical regimens
  • Emotional exhaustion from constant symptom management
  • The mental load of coordinating multiple specialists and treatments
  • Fluctuating symptoms that make planning impossible
  • Good days followed by crashes when you overdo it
  • The cognitive burden of remembering medication schedules, appointments, and symptom tracking
  • Grief cycles as each new diagnosis changes your life trajectory
  • Healthcare providers who only see their specialty, missing the bigger picture

üìÖ The daily reality:

  • Taking handfuls of medications multiple times per day
  • Juggling appointments with numerous specialists who don't communicate
  • Experiencing medication interactions and conflicting treatment advice
  • Symptom tracking to identify patterns and triggers across conditions

  • Constant triage of which symptoms need immediate attention
  • Fighting insurance battles for multiple conditions and treatments
  • Explaining your complex medical history repeatedly to new providers
